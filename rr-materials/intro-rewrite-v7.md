## Introduction

Organizational newcomers confront a quiet contradiction in their first months on the job. They are
expected to demonstrate competence to colleagues and supervisors who are still deciding whether
hiring them was the right call, yet they are also there to learn work they have not yet mastered
(Bauer et al. 2007, Bauer et al. 2025). These two demands pull in opposite directions. The surest
way to look competent is to make no mistakes; the surest way to learn is to make them, examine them,
and act differently the next time. How newcomers navigate this tension—whether their early errors
become evidence against them or raw material for development—shapes not only their immediate
performance but their trajectory in the organization for years afterward (Chen 2005,
Kammeyer-Mueller et al. 2013).

Socialization research has built a rich account of what readies newcomers to meet this challenge.
Individual dispositions such as learning goal orientation and proactive personality channel
newcomers' motivation to engage with difficult, unfamiliar work (Cooper-Thomas et al. 2014, Tan et
al. 2016, Gao et al. 2024), while relational climates—above all, psychological safety—give them the
security to take interpersonal risks and admit mistakes without fear of reprisal (Edmondson 1999,
Mukerjee and Metiu 2022, Montani et al. 2025). This work has taught us a great deal about why
newcomers are willing to commit and disclose errors. It has told us far less about what happens
next. An error, once committed and disclosed, is not self-interpreting feedback. To yield learning,
it must be diagnosed, traced to the action that produced it, and converted into a revised way of
working—and nothing about feeling safe or motivated guarantees that this conversion occurs. The
comfort to make an error is not the capacity to learn from it. Two newcomers equally willing to err
may differ sharply in whether their mistakes compound into competence or simply recur, and existing
accounts, trained on the conditions that *precede* an error, are largely silent on the
cognitive-procedural process that *follows* it.

We therefore ask a question the field has left open: once newcomers commit errors, what determines
whether those errors are converted into learning, and what observable signature does that conversion
leave? To answer it, we integrate action regulation theory (Frese and Zapf 1994, Frese and Keith
2015) with deviation-amplifying spiral logic (Maruyama 1963, Masuch 1985). Action regulation theory
explains the conversion at the level of a single error: a mistake forces a shift from routine
execution to higher-order regulation—reevaluating assumptions, restructuring strategy, and revising
action—but only when attention is turned toward analyzing the error rather than defending against
it. We focus on action errors, the unintended deviations from the goals, rules, and standards that
govern how a task is carried out (Van Dyck et al. 2005, Frese and Keith 2015). Action errors concern
the procedural quality of execution and are conceptually distinct from overall performance, the
holistic judgment of a newcomer's effectiveness in the role: the former is a proximal process, the
latter a distal outcome. The distinction matters, because it lets us treat the reduction of errors
not as a synonym for performance but as a visible trace of the regulatory learning that produces it.

Deviation-amplifying spiral logic adds the dimension action regulation theory has lacked: time. A
single conversion is one gain, but onboarding is a succession of them, and we argue these gains
compound. Early in entry, when a newcomer's grasp of the task is still thin, each error worked
through strengthens the very capacity that makes the next error easier to diagnose, so improvement
becomes self-fueling rather than merely additive. The result is a distinctive temporal form—an
accelerating decline in errors—that runs opposite to the decelerating curve of classic skill
acquisition, where the largest gains arrive first as a bounded skill is honed toward a ceiling
(Newell and Rosenbloom 1981, Argote et al. 2021). Whether this spiral takes hold, however, is not
automatic. We theorize that it is governed by the team's error culture. An error management culture,
which treats errors as occasions for analysis, frees newcomers to engage in the higher-order
regulation that fuels the spiral; an error aversion culture, which treats errors as threats to be
concealed, pulls attention toward self-protection and starves the spiral of fuel. And because errors
during entry are ambiguous events that demand sensemaking about their causes (Weick 1995, Maitlis
and Christianson 2014), we expect a newcomer's external locus of control—the tendency to attribute
outcomes to forces beyond one's control (Rotter 1966)—to condition how far a supportive culture is
actually taken up.

This reasoning carries an implication that reorders how newcomer potential should be read. If the
curvature of the error trajectory reflects compounding regulatory capacity, then how a newcomer's
errors *fall* should predict performance over and above how many errors *remain* at the end of
onboarding. Two newcomers may reach the same final error level, yet the one whose reduction
accelerated has demonstrated a transferable capacity that the other, sitting at a static level, has
not; velocity carries information that level cannot (Hsee and Abelson 1991, Johnson et al. 2013). We
test this model across two studies. In Study 1, we develop and content-validate a measure of
newcomer action error. In Study 2—a five-wave, four-source field study of 294 newcomer-team units in
a pharmaceutical firm—we measure team error culture through coworker ratings collected one month
*before* each newcomer arrives, establishing the team antecedent prior to entry, and then track
mentor-rated action errors across the first four months and leader-rated performance at the close of
onboarding.

Our study makes three contributions. First, we relocate where learning is won or lost in
socialization. The field has concentrated on the forces that make newcomers *ready* to learn—their
motivation and their safety; we show that these operate upstream of the moment learning actually
happens, and that a distinct, cognitive-procedural force—team error culture—governs whether a
surfaced error is converted into capability. Second, we extend action regulation theory by giving it
a temporal architecture. Integrating it with spiral logic lets us specify *when* error-based
learning gathers momentum rather than dissipates, distinguishing the accelerating dynamic of early
role learning from the decelerating dynamic of skill refinement—a developmental contingency the
theory has not previously articulated. Third, we reconceive the error trajectory itself as a signal
of learning. By separating action error from performance and showing that the trajectory's curvature
predicts performance beyond its endpoint, we offer scholars and managers a new way to read newcomer
potential: not by how few errors a newcomer makes, but by how fast those errors are falling. A
newcomer who begins rough but improves at an accelerating rate may be building deeper capacity than
one who starts clean and then plateaus.

## What changed, mapped to the five fixes

1. **Hook rebuilt around stakes (was 🔴).** Opens on the competence-vs-learning contradiction
   ("look competent = make no mistakes; learn = make them"), not the flat premise "entry is a
   learning process." Stakes are explicit and lead straight into the contribution.

2. **Problematization upgraded from gap to tension (was 🟡).** The pivot is now a genuine
   contradiction — *the comfort to make an error is not the capacity to learn from it* — rather than
   "this has received less attention." This directly disarms the R2/R3 line that the literature is
   richer than you implied: you concede the field's strength (readiness) and locate your distinct
   territory (conversion) beside it.

3. **Crisp standalone research question added (was 🟡).** One sentence: *"once newcomers commit
   errors, what determines whether those errors are converted into learning, and what observable
   signature does that conversion leave?"*

4. **Model paragraph split and design foreshadowed (was 🟡).** The overloaded paragraph is now two
   (single-error conversion + construct distinction; then the temporal spiral, culture, and
   moderator). A dedicated paragraph foreshadows the design — new action-error measure,
   **pre-entry coworker-rated culture**, multi-source waves — surfacing the empirical rigor that
   answers last round's main attack.

5. **Contributions pushed past the model to "so what" (was 🟡).** Each contribution now states what
   changes for the field; the third lands on the memorable reframe — *read potential by how fast
   errors fall, not by how few are made.*
