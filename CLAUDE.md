# Working in this repository

This repo is used to collaborate with the author on an academic **Revise & Resubmit for
*Organization Science*** (manuscript: *"Turning Errors into Productivity: How Team Error
Cultures Influence Newcomers' Error Trajectories and Onboarding"*). Source files and
working deliverables live in `rr-materials/`. Author-supplied writing rubrics:
`academic_paper_storyteller_prompt` (introductions) and `theory_craft_writer_prompt`
(theory).

## Standing principles for helping with the manuscript and response

These were learned from direct author feedback. Hold to them on every edit and evaluation.

1. **Change only for substantiated improvement.** Never rewrite or edit the author's prose
   for the sake of change. Default to preserving their text and their voice. Before
   proposing any edit, confirm it is genuinely better and state *specifically* why. Prefer
   surgical, minimal edits to wholesale rewrites; do a full rewrite only when explicitly
   asked. If a change is merely stylistic/lateral, say so — don't present it as necessary.

2. **Read before judging.** Never assert that the author's draft fails to address a point —
   or that a proposed alternative "covers everything" — without first reading the actual
   text closely. Do not claim coverage you have not verified against the words on the page.

3. **Evaluate in context, holistically.** Do not lift a sentence from the decision letter
   out of context and declare it must be addressed in a specific place (e.g., the
   introduction). Weigh the whole manuscript and the whole R&R toolkit. Many reviewer
   comments are best handled by **argument/clarification in the response letter**, not by
   changing the manuscript. Always distinguish "must revise the manuscript" from "address
   in the response letter," and consider how a change in one section interacts with the rest.

4. **Respect the positivist sequence — no post-hoc motivation.** Never use the study's own
   results to justify why the study should be done, or to motivate the hypotheses.
   Motivation and justification come **only from prior literature**. Theory and predictions
   come first and are then tested by the data.

5. **The author owns the decision and the voice.** Offer options with substantiated
   trade-offs; let the author choose. Do not impose changes.
